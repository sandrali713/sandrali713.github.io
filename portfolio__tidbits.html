Tidbits

24-hour Website Design

Dubhacks

Dubhacks is the Pacific Northwest's largest collegiate hackathon promoting building solutions for social good. It lasts for 24-hours, and is held at the University of Washington. The majority of students who apply are UW students, but people fly over from many places, like the University of British Columbia. There are three main tracks for projects: the classic track, which gives you freedom to work on whatever you'd like; the data track, which focuses on data analysis and visualization; and the community track, which lets you design solutions for a specific community.

Our Project

Prior to arriving at the event, I found a team. It wasn't required, but there's an active facebook page and paricipants are encouraged to use it to meet people. I planned to do it with one of my friends, a coder and a junior from UW. After making a post about our interests, we chatted with several people and joined another group of 2. Both were coders, a senior from UBC with Hackathon experience and a freshman from UW. We met up in person to brainstorm ideas before the event started in preparation so we could start coding and designing as soon as possible in our 24-hours.

The 4 of us had a lot of similar interests, like mental health awareness, accessibility, and climate change. We also wanted to use machine-learning in some aspect of our project, but were unsure of what part. After running through many options with all of these, we decided on something like an AI counselor/therapist. It could detect how you were feeling through facial expressions and speech, and give reccomendations for your behavior to make you feel better.

In addition to the 3 tracks, each of which have impressive prizes, there are a large number of sponsors, many who give away prizes for specific things as well. This list includes Microsoft, Google, Bloomberg, Blue Origin, Amazon, Facebook, and many more. We decided to go after the Microsoft prize, which was for the best use of Microsoft Azure. Azure fit our project needs the best and we thought we'd have a pretty good shot at winning the prize.

Initial Ideas

Based on the project we wanted to create and the community we wanted to serve, I did some user research to better understand who specifically would benefit from this product, and what specific needs they might have. We decided we didn't want to call our product a counselor or therapist because of the implications it could have, so we went for an AI journal or mood tracker instead. One of the potential users for this could be someone who struggles to write or type. As a looked into mobile impairments like this, and even watched a video of someone describing their life with these conditions, I began to understand that for many of these people, it was more than just a physical problem. The social anxiety, stress, and fatigue these physical conditions create were far less addressed and had no good solutions.

As I had more of an idea who our potential users were, I used a mind map to help me organize my thoughts, but it was constantly changing as the coders did research into what would be feasible to create in just 24-hours with the available data sets online. Then, based on what we agreed upon, I created the information architecture for our website. I went over this with our group as well, and after getting some feedback, I began to work on wireframes.

Initial Designs

Here are some of my initial wireframes and logo designs for the website. After getting feedback from my team, I did some iterations and tried some different designs. I also brainstormed names for the product/website, which was challenging but also a lot of fun. My teammates didn't have a lot of feedback concerning the design aspect, but mostly the functionality and feasibility. After we settled on a logo design and the wireframes, I started designing and developing on the computer.

Challenges

There were a lot of challenges during this project. One of the big ones was the time constraint. We had time to prepare for it, so we tried to keep our scope small, but we had our eyes on the prize and wanted to do something that would wow the judges. The hardest part about the time constraint was that I had to design before the coding aspect of the project was finished - in fact, nearly everything was done at the same time. Because of this, the design had to keep changing based on what the coders could figure out. I would make sitemaps and wireframes that we thought would work, and the the code would change and I'd have to design something new on the fly. Many of these changes were also last minute, so the design was rushed and not as finalized as I would've liked it to be.

Another big challenge was being the only designer on the team. I didn't get much feedback for a lot of the designs - they sometimes had opinions on how things look, but couldn't tell me why they thought that way or what they think should be done differently. It was a very new experience to work with a team of all coders with no design experience, but it really opened my eyes and I learned a lot through it.

Building and then designing an entire website was also a challenge for me. I have a bit of web development experience, but to build an entire one in 24-hours including the logo, themes, images, and functionality was a bit of a struggle. The others helped me occasionally when they had the time, but the coding was intense and they were primarily focused on that. I had to learn what to prioritize and what to improvise, and how to write and upload code when working on a team. It was an incredibly fast-paced but also very educational and rewarding experience, and I plan on participating in future hackathons as well.
