Caire

User Research

The Story

Caire is an app that was created by 4 people: Malik Haythorne, Amelia Wang, Kelsey Kua and me. It was a quarter-long project for an HCDE user-design class. We brainstormed users we knew nothing about, and picked one to research and design for. We decided to go with child care workers, because they have one of the highest burn-out rates of all jobs, and we were curious why that is.

Brief

Our goal for this project was to understand their user to the point where we could design something that would make their lives easier - something they might not even know they needed, but once they had, it would ease their burden.

In order to understand our users' struggles, values, and goals, we interviewed 4 different people in the child care field. We learned what an average day looks like, what their responsiblities are, what causes them sadness and grief and stress, and what brightens their day and makes it all worthwhile.

Personas

Based on the data from our interviews, we created 2 personas of potential users. We used their thoughts, attitudes, and behaviors and tried to minimize making assumptions to avoid stereotyping behaviors and beliefs. Our two personas can be seen to the left, and some of the highlights we found in our research include:

  • Workers are incredibly motivated by giving back to their community and helping clients receive justice
  • An unpredictable schedule is not uncommon as emergent referrals pop up frequently
  • Handling cases with sensitive information inflicts an emotional burden on workers

"Often people aren’t in a position to make big changes in their lives until they have the basic needs of shelter and safety.""

Cathy Walker, a persona of a child care worker based off our interviews of real workers

User Journey Map

We then mapped out a user journey map for an average day of a child social care worker, including their highs and lows. We added thoughts and actions to see what would lead them to have varying stress and motivation levels. From here, we can start to identify their pain-points and brainstorm what we could build to lessen their burden.

Storyboards

To brainstorm solutions, we created storyboards to imagine how our user could interact with an app, website, or physical product. Our main takeaways were that we wanted our solution to include:

  • An automated scheduling component to allow users to accommodate emergency appointments without feeling overwhelmed
  • A self-care aspect to remind them to eat lunch and take breaks
  • A messaging board to allow them to feel more connected with their coworkers

Information Architecture

Next, we created an information architecture to map out all the things we wanted to include in our app/website, and grouped them in ways we thought they should be organized. High-Fidelity Mockups

We picked 3 screens from our wireframes to make high-fidelity mockups of. The first one we chose was the home screen, which shows your day at a glance with limiting hours and sections for lunch and breaks. There are also colored dots to indicate the priority of events in case not everything can be done.


The second is the calendar screen, which gives you an idea of how busy each day will be and lets you easily rearrange tasks. Additonally, when a task is added, the program finds the best options to add it in, while considering priority and your workload.


The third is the health dashboard, which tracks your progress of self-care tasks, and ensures you to take good care of yourself. It gives you reminders if you haven't done one in a while, and small rewards for being consistent.

Reflection

Overall, it was a really fun and rewarding project! It truly taught me the importance of user-centered design in this world and gave a first-hand preview of what projects in industry can be like.


Additionally, I learned was that small decisions add up and can greatly impact the design process later on. An instance of this occurred early on in our project right after user interviews. As we progressed through the user-centered process, some of us found that we needed to ask our users more questions about issues not covered in our first (and only) round of interview questions. This may have been a problem due to the context in which this project was created - a ten-week class in small groups of 4 for an undergraduate course.


Although we conducted user research at the beginning of our project, real social workers saw no other part of our design process. Their feedback would have been helpful at many times in the project, such as during paper prototyping and critiques. If we did this project again, we would want to get more input from social workers throughout our design process, not just during research. This would encourage our project to be practical and effective for our user.
